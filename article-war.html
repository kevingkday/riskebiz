What Businesses Should Know About War Exclusions

Traditional war exclusion clauses, designed for physical battlefield damage, are being aggressively updated by global reinsurance markets to exclude state-sponsored cyber operations.

For centuries, commercial insurance contracts have contained standard **War Risks Exclusions**. Underwriters exclude war because military actions cause catastrophic, correlated losses that threaten the solvency of insurance companies.

Historically, applying this exclusion was straightforward. If a factory was destroyed by an artillery shell or seized by an invading army, the exclusion triggered, and the claim was denied. However, the rise of state-sponsored cyber attacks has made this exclusion a major source of litigation in commercial insurance.

The NotPetya Turning Point

The debate reached a turning point following the 2017 **NotPetya malware** attack. Designed by state-sponsored actors to target Ukrainian infrastructure, the malware spread globally, causing billions of dollars in business interruption losses for multi-national corporations.

When Merck, a global pharmaceutical firm, claimed $1.4 billion in losses under its property insurance package, its insurers denied the claim. They argued that because NotPetya was an act of state-sponsored hostility, it fell under the war exclusion. Merck sued and won, with the courts ruling that traditional war exclusions only apply to physical hostilities, not digital data destruction.

"The Merck ruling sent shockwaves through the reinsurance markets. Underwriters realized their physical war exclusions were failing to protect them from systemic cyber risks."

The New Lloyd's Mandate

In response, **Lloyd's of London** issued a mandate requiring all syndicates to write distinct cyber war exclusions. These clauses must explicitly exclude coverage for cyber operations carried out by, or on behalf of, a sovereign state.

The challenge for businesses is **attribution**. Proving that a hack was directed by a foreign state—rather than an independent cybercriminal syndicate—is technically complex. Modern policy language is shifting the burden of proof, allowing insurers to deny claims if a government agency attributes the cyber attack to a foreign nation.

How CFOs Should Respond

To navigate this shifting legal landscape, corporate risk officers should:

  • Review Cyber Policy Definitions: Identify what constitutes an "act of war" or a "state-sponsored cyber operation" in your policy. Look for clauses that require formal attribution.
  • Secure Write-Back Clauses: Negotiate endorsements that write back coverage for collateral damage—where your business is affected by a state-sponsored attack but was not the primary target.
  • Diversify Coverages: Combine standalone cyber policies with specialized **Political Risk** and **Political Violence** programs.
