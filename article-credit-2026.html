Export Credit Insurance vs. Letters of Credit (2026 Edition)

Originally published in CanadExport magazine in 1999, "Export Credit Insurance: An Alternative to Letters of Credit" discussed the commercial constraints of Letters of Credit for Canadian exporters. Today, global supply chains demand even faster turnaround times, making open-account credit insurance more critical than ever.

1. The Commercial Shift to Open Account

In 1999, Letters of Credit (LCs) were still standard practice for international transactions. Today, however, open-account trading terms have become the baseline expectation in global commerce. Buyers are highly resistant to tying up their corporate credit lines to issue LCs, nor do they want to absorb the associated bank fees. Suppliers who insist on LCs frequently lose out to international competitors willing to offer open credit terms.

For a Canadian company seeking to expand, offering open-account terms is a significant sales advantage, but doing so without protection exposes accounts receivable to catastrophic loss. Export Credit Insurance (also known as Trade Credit Insurance, or TCI) bridges this gap. It allows suppliers to meet buyer demands for open terms while ensuring their balance sheet remains protected against default.

2. The Operational Friction of Letters of Credit

While LCs provide high payment security, they introduce severe operational friction:

  • Working Capital Constraints: An LC requires the buyer's bank to freeze a corresponding portion of the buyer's credit line. This limits their purchasing capacity and slows growth.
  • Documentation Discrepancies: LCs are notoriously rigid. Minor typos or discrepancies in shipping documents (such as bills of lading, certificates of origin, or invoices) can cause banks to reject the documents, causing payment delays and bank renegotiation fees.
  • High Transaction Costs: Issuing, advising, amending, and confirming LCs incurs bank fees on both sides, reducing the net profit margins of the sale.
"In modern commerce, requiring a Letter of Credit forces the buyer to arrange and pay for your security. Trade Credit Insurance shifts that security to your own policy, leaving the buyer's credit lines free."

3. Credit Insurance as a Strategic Sales Tool

TCI acts as an active sales driver rather than a passive expense. Because the insurance covers up to 90% of the receivable value against default (due to insolvency, protracted non-payment, or political transfer blocks), exporters can confidently offer larger credit limits and longer payment terms (e.g. 60 or 90 days instead of 30 days) to new and existing buyers.

In many cases, the foreign buyer is completely unaware that their account is insured. The relationship is maintained on standard open-account invoicing, preserving commercial goodwill and accelerating transaction speed.

4. Boosting Receivables Financing

Another key advantage of TCI in 2026 is its role in trade finance. Canadian commercial banks and specialty export lenders (including EDC) are far more likely to include foreign receivables in an exporter's borrowing base if they are backed by credit insurance. By assigning the policy benefits to the lender, exporters can secure higher margining rates (often up to 85-90% of foreign receivables) and lower borrowing costs, significantly boosting corporate cash flow.

5. Designing a Custom Program

Exporters can select from several policy structures to align with their commercial profile:

  • Whole Turnover Policies: Insures all credit sales across the entire customer portfolio, providing the lowest premium rates.
  • Key Buyer / Selected Accounts: Covers only the largest or highest-risk accounts, leaving stable domestic buyers self-insured.
  • Single Buyer Coverage: Insures a single, high-value transaction or client (common for large-scale capital exports).
