Trade Credit Insurance for Exporters

For many exporters, accounts receivable constitute up to forty percent of total corporate assets. Leaving this exposure uninsured exposes the entire enterprise to catastrophic cash flow crises.

Exporters regularly ship products across borders and wait 60 to 90 days for payments. During this period, the exporter faces significant counterparty credit risk. If the foreign buyer goes bankrupt or encounters a cash liquidity crisis, the exporter is left holding significant unpaid invoices.

**Trade Credit Insurance (TCI)** mitigates this exposure. Under TCI policies, the insurer indemnifies the exporter for a pre-determined percentage of the outstanding debt—typically 85% to 95%—if the customer fails to pay.

Dual Coverages: Commercial & Political Risks

A structured Export TCI program covers two distinct classes of non-payment:

  • Commercial Risks: This covers standard customer defaults, including formal insolvencies, liquidations, bankruptcies, or protracted defaults where the customer simply fails to pay within the agreed terms.
  • Political Risks: Covers non-payments resulting from actions by a sovereign government. Examples include currency conversion blocks (preventing the buyer from securing dollars or euros), government export bans, war or civil conflicts in the buyer's region, or sovereign debt cancellations.
"Trade Credit Insurance is not just a risk mitigation program; it is a critical growth tool that allows Canadian exporters to offer competitive open account terms to foreign buyers."

Strategic Funding Benefits

Beyond risk transfer, TCI offers substantial credit and financing benefits. Commercial banks and export financiers (like EDC) are far more willing to lend against foreign accounts receivable if they are backed by credit insurance. Exporters can secure higher borrowing caps and better interest terms, improving working capital.
