Reducing the Risk in Risky Business (2026 Edition)

Originally published in CanadExport magazine in 1997, "Reducing the Risk in Risky Business" discussed political risk insurance for Canadian companies investing overseas. Nearly three decades later, geopolitical uncertainty has made these risks even more important.

1. The New Era of Geopolitical Friction

In 1997, globalization was moving forward at a rapid pace. The post-Cold War era promised expanding trade routes, developing legal frameworks, and integrating capital markets. In that environment, political risk insurance was often treated as a niche product, purchased primarily by mining conglomerates or infrastructure builders operating in high-volatility emerging economies.

Today, that integration has fractured. We live in a multi-polar environment characterized by economic weaponization, regional conflicts, and supply chain vulnerabilities. The war in Ukraine has reshaped European energy and agricultural security, while Middle East instability regularly disrupts major maritime lanes. Geopolitical friction is no longer confined to specific developing regions; it is a systemic concern that affects standard corporate operations worldwide.

2. Evolution of the Risk Matrix

While the fundamental categories of political risk remain, the ways they manifest have changed significantly over the last thirty years:

  • Sanctions and Trade Restrictions: Rather than physical blockades, modern geopolitical conflict is often waged via compliance audits, asset freezes, and export restrictions. Navigating these overlapping regulatory structures requires active contractual monitoring.
  • Physical Sabotage and Drone Warfare: Industrial installations, refineries, and container ports face new physical threats. UAVs (drones) allow hostile actors to target assets from long distances, bypassing traditional local security measures.
  • State-Sponsored Cyber Hostilities: Critical utilities, logistics portals, and manufacturing lines face disruption from state-aligned hacker groups. These attacks can freeze operations without causing physical damage, rendering standard business interruption policies ineffective.
"In the modern trade environment, regulatory sanctions and cyber interruptions can shut down overseas operations just as effectively as physical expropriation."

3. Traditional Exclusions in Commercial Policies

Standard commercial insurance policies (Commercial Property and Commercial General Liability) are built to cover predictable, domestic risks like localized fires, water damage, or slip-and-fall incidents. To maintain their capital limits, standard insurers include broad, absolute exclusions for:

  • Acts of war, invasion, and hostile operations (whether declared or not)
  • Acts of terrorism, civil unrest, and general sabotage
  • Hostile cyber operations attributed to sovereign states
  • Government interventions, including confiscations, expropriations, and nationalizations

As a result, a business relying on standard commercial coverage faces significant exposure when operating assets or executing contracts in volatile regions.

4. Key Coverages to Secure

A structured Political Risk Insurance (PRI) architecture must be designed to cover the following operational vulnerabilities:

  • Government Confiscation & Expropriation: Covers the loss of physical equipment, inventory, or facilities due to direct government seizure or discriminatory regulatory actions that prevent operation.
  • Currency Inconvertibility: Triggers when a host government blocks the conversion of local currency into foreign currency (USD, CAD, EUR) or halts the outward transfer of capital.
  • Contract Frustration: Protects receivables and investments when a public buyer defaults, cancels a license, or fails to honor an agreement, or when trade sanctions prevent contract execution.
  • Political Violence: Insures physical property against damage caused by war, civil conflict, rebellion, revolution, or targeted sabotage.

5. Navigating Global Specialty Markets

Because political risk is correlated and volatile, it cannot be covered in standard domestic markets. Accessing coverage requires specialized global underwriting markets that possess the capacity and expertise to write these complex risk structures.

Securing coverage requires detailed technical submissions, including third-party security audits, contract reviews, and clear risk mitigation procedures.
