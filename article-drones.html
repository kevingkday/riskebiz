Does Commercial Property Insurance Cover Drone Attacks?

As drone incidents scale globally, risk managers are discovering that physical damages caused by Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) fall into a complex gray zone between vandalism and military/terrorism exclusions.

The proliferation of commercial and hobbyist drones has introduced a new vector of threat for industrial complexes, warehouses, and shipping terminals. When a drone collides with an asset, causes a fire, or acts as a hostile intelligence collector, companies automatically assume their standard **Commercial Property (CP)** policy will cover the damage.

However, standard policies were written before UAVs became common. As a result, insurers are using existing exclusions to deny claims, creating significant policy gaps for corporate policyholders.

The Exclusion Intersection

A drone collision or payload explosion typically triggers three standard exclusions:

  • Aviation & Aircraft Exclusions: Most standard CGL and Property policies contain strict exclusions for any damage caused by "aircraft". In insurance law, a drone is categorized as an unmanned aircraft. Unless specifically endorsed, damage directly resulting from aircraft contact is excluded.
  • Terrorism Exclusions: If the drone attack is determined to be ideologically, politically, or religiously motivated, standard property coverage is void under the Terrorism Exclusion. Companies must hold specific TRIA (in the US) or specialized Sabotage and Terrorism policies to claim.
  • War Risks Exclusions: If the drone is launched by a state actor or aligned military faction (even in non-declared conflicts), the damage is categorized under 'acts of war' and is excluded.
"Because drones can be operated anonymously, proving whether an incident was simple civil vandalism or an act of structured sabotage is one of the most contentious issues in modern commercial claims."

Mitigating the Exposure

To secure protection, companies must purchase **Aviation Liability** policies that explicitly cover Unmanned Aerial Systems (UAS), or secure specialized **Sabotage & Terrorism** property endorsements that write back the exclusions found in standard packages. Risk managers should audit their policies to verify where physical damage from UAVs is designated.
